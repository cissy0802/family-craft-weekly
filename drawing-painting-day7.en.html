Doing Together: Drawing & Painting

2026-06-19 · Day 7
This one needs almost no prep: a few sheets of paper, a box of crayons or watercolors, some newspaper on the table, and you're off. But drawing is also the easiest thing for a grown-up to ruin—one "why is your sun green?" and the pencil box snaps shut. So there's only one rule this time: no judging, no correcting, no showing her the "right answer." We break it into four things: scribble freely, sketch outdoors, paint one big picture together, and run a color-mixing experiment. The first two build "daring to draw" and "really looking"; the last two build cooperation and "why." Whether it looks like anything doesn't matter at all—what matters is that she wants to keep going. Five minutes of setup, drop the words "draw it well," and begin.

1. Scribble Freely, No Judging涂鸦不评判

⏱ 15–30 minMaterials: paper + pens

Prep & Safety

Materials: big sheets of paper (the back of an old calendar, printer paper taped together), crayons or markers. The game is give a starting point, never a right answer: draw a wobbly line, a circle, or an ink blot, then say "What does this look like? Turn it into something." She continues, making that line into a snake, a mountain, an elephant's trunk—whatever she wants. Take turns: she draws a stroke, you add one, and see what strange thing the page becomes. Throughout, only ask, never grade—"Who's this?" "Where's it going?"—and never say "that's wrong / it doesn't look right."

Safety: a 3-year-old will chew pens and stuff caps in her mouth, so choose non-toxic, washable crayons/markers, and don't let her play with loose caps (choking risk). Keep ink pens and permanent markers away from the 3-year-old—they end up on hands, faces, even mouths. Lay newspaper or an old sheet down first, put her in clothes that can get dirty, and tape a big sheet to the wall as the "draw-anywhere spot" so she doesn't redecorate your wall.

Age 3

Her job is bold scribbling—at this age a drawing is naturally lines and color blocks, so don't expect recognizable things. Give her big paper and fat crayons and let her swipe, circle, and stab dots—any way is right. What you do is crouch down and marvel together: "Wow, all that red!" Put the attention on the action and the color, not on "what is it." If she says "that's Mommy," then it's Mommy—never argue.

Age 9

At her age kids start caring whether it "looks right," which can make them afraid to draw. Challenge her to draw "wrong" on purpose: draw a cat with her eyes closed, draw her own hand without looking at the paper (blind contour), or draw a person with only three lines. The point is to free her from "getting it right" and make drawing fun again. She can also be the "starting-point designer," handing you and her sister wobbly lines to turn into things.

Both Sisters

Play "drawing relay": the 9-year-old draws an opening (a head, a line), the 3-year-old adds a stroke, back to the 9-year-old, taking turns one stroke at a time until the page is full. The 9-year-old handles ideas and the main shapes, the 3-year-old handles colors and decoration (no swapping: composition and main lines to big sister, color blocks and dots to little sister). At the end, name your "mixed-up creature" together—the sillier the better.

The Why + a Twist

Why not correct a child's drawing? Because to a child, drawing isn't "reproducing reality"—it's "expressing what's in her head." A green sun, purple hair, a cat bigger than the house: those are her real thoughts. Correct them into "right" and what she draws is no longer her own. Twist: play "music scribble"—put on a song and let her draw lines along with it: fast music, fast jagged lines; slow music, soft curves. Her hand "dances" to the sound.

2. Sketching Outdoors户外写生

⏱ 20–30 minMaterials: pad + pen

Prep & Safety

Materials: a notebook or paper on a hard board, one pen (pencil or marker). Take the kids downstairs, to a park, or even just the balcony, and find something to draw from life: a leaf, a flower, a perched bird, a building in the distance. The point of sketching isn't accuracy—it's "actually looking." Normally we look at things just to "recognize" them (oh, a leaf); sketching forces you to see detail: is the edge toothed or smooth? How many veins? That slow looking is the whole point. Draw ten minutes—the looking should take longer than the drawing.

Safety: outdoors, the usual rules—keep the 3-year-old in sight, away from roads and water, and remind everyone "we don't pick or eat unknown plants/berries" (some are toxic or thorny). In summer, sun and bug protection, bring water. Wash hands after handling leaves or stones you brought back as "models" before eating. A sharpened pencil tip is a small sharp object for a 3-year-old—pass it tip-down.

Age 3

She can't really "draw from life" yet, and that's fine—her job is collector and colorer: help gather a few pretty leaves and stones as "models," then put some colors and shapes on paper to stand for them. Or even simpler—put a leaf under the paper and rub a crayon back and forth over it, and the leaf's veins "print" through (rubbing). She'll love this one: easy, and a built-in surprise.

Age 9

Her job is to really sketch one thing—pick a leaf or a flower and challenge herself to look for a full minute before the pen touches paper, then get as much detail as she can (veins, teeth, a bug-hole, color shifts). Step up: draw the same leaf twice, once for outline, once for detail, and compare which "looks more right." She can add a couple of observation notes beside it (what tree, how the leaf feels) to make a real nature-sketch page.

Both Sisters

Make a "what we saw today" page together: the 9-year-old is lead artist, drawing what they saw and labeling it; the 3-year-old is collector and rubbing-maker, gathering materials and adding leaf rubbings to the same page (no swapping: sketching and notes to big sister, gathering and rubbing to little sister). That one page holds big sister's careful observation and little sister's real-leaf prints—a true "our nature record."

The Why + a Twist

Why do you remember a thing better after drawing it? Because drawing forces the brain to take a thing apart—what shape, how many parts, how they connect—and that "seeing into it" goes far deeper than snapping a photo. Da Vinci understood the world by sketching it nonstop. Twist: draw the same tree or potted plant for several days running and watch what changes over a week (taller, blooming, leaves yellowing)—turn sketching into a little observation diary.

3. One Big Painting, Together合作大画

⏱ 30 min+Materials: big paper + paint

Prep & Safety

Materials: a sheet of paper as big as possible (printer sheets taped together, a flattened shipping box, the back of an old calendar), watercolors or markers. The game is for the whole family to paint one big scene on the same sheet—say "our city," "the underwater world," or "a monster amusement park." Agree on a theme first, then split up: someone paints houses, someone cars, someone the sun, and it all joins into one scene. The point is the cooperation of sharing one sheet: your house has to leave room for my road, my river has to flow down from the foot of your mountain.

Safety: use kid-safe, non-toxic, washable paints. A 3-year-old will smear paint-covered hands on her mouth and face, so keep a damp cloth nearby and watch that she doesn't drink the paint water. Lay newspaper or plastic on the floor, set the paint-water cups somewhere stable so they don't tip. Old clothes or a smock worn backward. Any step that needs scissors or breaking down a box is for grown-ups—kids don't touch the blade.

Age 3

Her job is large areas of "base coat" and handprints—exactly her strength: brush a wide stretch of blue for sky/sea, dip a palm in paint and stamp a row of handprints as little fish/trees/clouds. She doesn't need to paint anything specific; spreading color and stamping prints are big, safe, satisfying moves—and they lay down the background that big sister adds detail on top of.

Age 9

Her job is planning and the main subjects—discuss with everyone how to divide the sheet and what goes where (composition), then paint the main things (the city's towers, the big whale in the sea). Challenge: give the picture "near and far"—near things bigger and lower, far things smaller and higher, trying to create a sense of space. She also coordinates her little sister: "this patch of sky is yours to brush," learning to delegate instead of doing it all.

Both Sisters

This activity is cooperation by design: the 9-year-old is "lead designer + main subjects," the 3-year-old is "base coat + handprint decorations" (no swapping: composition and detail to big sister, big color blocks and stamps to little sister). Agree on one rule: nobody paints over what someone else drew—ask first before adding. Each sister claims half the world; together it becomes a whole one, hung on the wall as the family's piece.

The Why + a Twist

Why paint on one shared sheet instead of each on her own? Because sharing a sheet forces a child to consider "the other person's drawing"—leaving room, connecting lines—and that's cooperation: not everyone doing their own thing, but making the separate parts add up to one whole. Twist: paint a "long road"—roll out a strip of wide paper (or several taped together) on the floor and draw the route from home to school, adding everything along the way, then lay it down the hallway like a map.

4. Color Mixing Experiment颜色混合实验

⏱ 20 minMaterials: 3 paint colors

Prep & Safety

Materials: just red, yellow, and blue paint (watercolor, poster paint, or water with food coloring), a few small cups/a palette, white paper. The game is to "make" new colors with your own hands: what does red + yellow make? Yellow + blue? Red + blue? Have the child guess first, then mix, and for each new color dab a stroke on paper and write "red + yellow = orange." Step up: add a little white to a color bit by bit and watch it lighten step by step, mixing a whole row of shades of one color.

Safety: again use non-toxic, washable paint; mixing with food coloring + water is safest (no big harm if the 3-year-old tastes a drop, but still say clearly "this is for playing, not drinking"). Label each cup and never mix them in with real drinking cups. Color water stains hands—keep wipes nearby. Don't let the 3-year-old hold paint caps / coloring bottles alone (swallowing risk); you squeeze it out, she mixes.

Age 3

Her job is "stirring" and "witnessing the magic"—you squeeze/drop two colors into one cup and she stirs hard with a little spoon/stick, watching the two colors "poof" into a new one. Her eyes go wide at that magic moment. Let her dab the new color onto paper—the fun is the pride of "I stirred that!" Don't ask her to remember recipes; playing with the color is plenty.

Age 9

Her job is to run the experiment and record—like a little scientist: first guess what red + yellow makes, then mix to check, and write down both "guessed" and "real" on paper. Challenge: mix a specific color, like "autumn orange" or "grass green," figuring out the ratio yourself (what if I add more yellow?). Bigger question: why can three colors make so many? (the three-primary principle of light and pigment.)

Both Sisters

The 9-year-old is the "recipe maker," deciding which two to mix, how much of each, and recording results; the 3-year-old is the "stirrer," mixing it smooth and dabbing the result into the right box on the paper (no swapping: recipes and records to big sister, stirring and dabbing to little sister). Together make a "color map": line up every color you mixed into a swatch chart, from the three primaries to all the blends—more and more—and tape it to the wall as a trophy.

The Why + a Twist

Red, yellow, and blue are the "primary colors"—they can't be mixed from anything else, yet two at a time they make orange, green, and purple, and mixing on you can make nearly every color. All those colors in your paint box are basically these three combined. Twist: play "milk fireworks"—pour a thin layer of milk in a plate, drop in a few food colorings, then touch a dish-soap-tipped cotton swab to it and the colors "explode" and dart around (that's the Day 1 milk rainbow—nice review): part painting, part science.

Pick Just One This Week

If you'll do only one, the easiest is the color-mixing experiment—red, yellow, blue, a few cups, one sheet of paper, set up in five minutes. It stitches "drawing" to "why": the 3-year-old stirs and watches the magic, the 9-year-old guesses recipes and records, little sister stirs while big sister writes—seamless teamwork. No "does it look right" pressure, every mix a genuine surprise, and a swatch chart on the wall is the result. Lowest effort, most reliable "wow"—the easiest, most delightful way into this week.

The process matters more than the result; being there matters more than teaching.
